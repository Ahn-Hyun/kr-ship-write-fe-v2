### FAQ

**Why does a sudden crude oil plunge benefit South Korean airlines but hurt domestic refiners?**
A sudden drop in crude oil prices directly reduces the operating expenses for airlines, as jet fuel typically accounts for about 30% of their total costs. This immediate cost relief widens their margin spread, creating powerful operating leverage. Conversely, domestic refiners purchase crude oil weeks in advance. When prices plunge rapidly, the expensive crude they already hold in inventory loses value compared to the current market price of refined products, forcing them to record massive "inventory valuation losses" and severely hurting their short-term net income.

**How will the recent drop in WTI prices impact KEPCO and other South Korean utility margins?**
As South Korea is a net energy importer, utilities like KEPCO are highly sensitive to global commodity prices. The recent drop in WTI prices significantly lowers the input costs for power generation fuels. This structural reduction in Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) relieves the political and economic pressure to hike electricity rates, paving a clear path for margin recovery and improved free cash flow for these state-led entities.

**Does the 11.45% drop in oil prices signal a recession risk for South Korea's export-driven economy?**
It is a valid concern. While lower oil prices generally benefit energy importers, an extreme 11.45% plunge can act as a leading indicator of severe global "demand destruction." If the drop is driven by rapidly shrinking global consumption rather than supply relief, South Korea's export-heavy economy (reliant on semiconductors, autos, and petrochemicals) could face massive macroeconomic headwinds that would easily outweigh the benefits of cheaper energy imports. However, concrete real-economy data (like PMI) is needed to confirm this downside scenario.

**How quickly will lower jet fuel prices translate into restored capacity for low-cost carriers like T'way and Jeju Air?**
There is typically a one-month lag for jet fuel price changes to fully reflect in an airline's actual earnings. Low-cost carriers (LCCs) like T'way and Jeju Air, which previously cut capacity on low-demand routes due to soaring fixed costs, will likely begin restoring flight capacity as soon as this one-month lag passes and the reduced fuel costs officially ease their cash flow pressures, providing a strong signal of industry bottoming out.
